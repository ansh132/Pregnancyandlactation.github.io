Safe Medicines in Pregnancy & Breastfeeding

A practical guide to common over-the-counter medicines and herbal products, designed to help you make safe, informed choices.

⚠️ Important: This guide provides general information only. Always speak with your pharmacist or doctor before taking any medicine during pregnancy or while breastfeeding.

Key principles of self-care
What every pregnant or breastfeeding patient should know
🤰

Use the minimum effective dose

Take the lowest dose that relieves your symptoms, for the shortest time needed.

⏱️

Timing matters

Some medicines are safer in certain trimesters. Risks can differ across early, mid and late pregnancy.

🌿

Natural ≠ safe

Herbal products can also affect your baby. Always tell your pharmacist what herbal products you use.

💬

Ask your pharmacist first

Community pharmacists are trained to advise on medicine safety during pregnancy and breastfeeding.

About TGA pregnancy categories

Australia uses TGA pregnancy categories (A, B1–B3, C, D, X) as a starting point for guidance. However, these categories are primarily designed for health professionals and do not account for trimester-specific risks, dose, or individual circumstances. They should not be used as the sole source of advice. Always seek a full counselling conversation with your pharmacist or doctor.

Nausea & vomiting of pregnancy (NVP)

Affects up to 80% of pregnant women. Lifestyle measures (small frequent meals, ginger, avoiding triggers) should be tried first. Refer to a GP if vomiting is severe or persistent (hyperemesis gravidarum).

Ginger
Tablets, tea, biscuits
Generally safe
🤰 PregEvidence supports ginger (up to 1 g/day) for mild-to-moderate NVP. Considered safe at culinary and therapeutic doses.
🤱 BFGenerally considered safe in food amounts. Limited data on high-dose supplementation.
Ginger ale, crystallised ginger, and ginger biscuits are low-risk options for mild symptoms.
Doxylamine + pyridoxine
e.g. Mergodoxine (Rx in AU)
Refer to GP
🤰 PregCommonly used combination for NVP. Considered first-line pharmacological treatment. Requires a prescription in Australia.
🤱 BFDoxylamine may reduce milk supply and cause drowsiness in the infant. Generally avoided during breastfeeding.
Pharmacists should refer to a GP for prescription-level antiemetics if lifestyle measures fail.
Antacids for nausea
e.g. Mylanta
Generally safe
🤰 PregAluminium/magnesium-based antacids are generally considered safe in pregnancy when used short-term. Avoid sodium bicarbonate products.
🤱 BFMinimal systemic absorption; considered compatible with breastfeeding.

Reflux & heartburn in pregnancy

Very common — affects up to 80% of pregnant women, particularly in the third trimester. Lifestyle advice (small meals, avoid lying down after eating, elevate head of bed) should be recommended first.

Antacids
Mylanta, Gaviscon, Quick-Eze
First line
🤰 PregCalcium carbonate and magnesium-based antacids are the preferred first line. Gaviscon (alginate-based) is particularly suitable. Avoid sodium bicarbonate (excess sodium load).
🤱 BFMinimal systemic absorption. All common antacids considered compatible with breastfeeding.
Avoid antacids containing aspirin (e.g. Alka-Seltzer) during pregnancy and breastfeeding.
Ranitidine / Famotidine
H2 blockers
Use with caution
🤰 PregFamotidine is generally preferred over ranitidine (which was withdrawn). Considered low risk when antacids are insufficient — TGA Category B1.
🤱 BFFamotidine is preferred as it has lower milk transfer than cimetidine. Short-term use considered acceptable.
PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole)
e.g. Losec, Somac
GP referral preferred
🤰 PregGenerally reserved for severe reflux not responding to antacids/H2 blockers. Pantoprazole and omeprazole are most studied. Refer to GP.
🤱 BFLow transfer into milk; omeprazole and pantoprazole are considered compatible. Used when benefit outweighs risk.

Constipation

Common in pregnancy due to hormonal changes and iron supplementation. Recommend increased fluid intake, dietary fibre, and physical activity as first steps.

Bulk-forming laxatives
Psyllium husk (Metamucil), ispaghula
First line
🤰 PregSafe throughout pregnancy. Not absorbed systemically. Important to take with plenty of water.
🤱 BFNot systemically absorbed. Compatible with breastfeeding.
Take with at least 200 mL of water to prevent oesophageal obstruction.
Osmotic laxatives
Lactulose, Movicol, Osmolax
Generally safe
🤰 PregMacrogol (Movicol/Osmolax) and lactulose are considered safe. Minimal systemic absorption.
🤱 BFCompatible with breastfeeding. Not absorbed into breast milk in significant amounts.
Stimulant laxatives
Senna, bisacodyl (Dulcolax)
Use with caution
🤰 PregShort-term use generally considered acceptable when other measures fail. Avoid in first trimester if possible and avoid prolonged use.
🤱 BFSenna passes minimally into breast milk; short-term use considered acceptable. Monitor infant for loose stools.
Docusate (stool softener)
e.g. Coloxyl
Limited evidence
🤰 PregLimited safety data. Avoid in first trimester. Preferred laxatives (bulk-forming, osmotic) should be trialled first.
🤱 BFMinimal data. Short-term use considered low risk but prefer alternatives with more evidence.

Colds, cough & nasal congestion

Most cold symptoms are self-limiting. Saline nasal spray, steam inhalation, honey (in adults), and rest are all suitable first-line options without any medicine risk.

Saline nasal sprays
e.g. Flo Saline, Nasonex Saline
Safe and preferred
🤰 PregCompletely safe. No systemic absorption. Recommended as first-line for nasal congestion.
🤱 BFSafe. No concerns.
Decongestants (oral)
Pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine
Generally avoid
🤰 PregAvoid — particularly in the first trimester. Pseudoephedrine has been associated with abdominal wall defects. Avoid in all trimesters where possible.
🤱 BFPseudoephedrine can reduce milk supply and passes into breast milk. Best avoided during breastfeeding.
Use saline sprays or steam inhalation instead. Short-term intranasal decongestants (e.g. oxymetazoline) may be preferable to oral decongestants if absolutely needed — discuss with pharmacist.
Oxymetazoline (nasal)
e.g. Drixine, Otrivin
Short-term only
🤰 PregLower systemic absorption than oral decongestants. Short-term use (max 3 days) may be considered if nasal congestion significantly affects sleep or eating. Avoid prolonged use.
🤱 BFLow systemic absorption; generally considered preferable to oral decongestants for short-term use.
Cough syrups (dextromethorphan)
e.g. Robitussin DM
Use with caution
🤰 PregLimited data. Generally avoided in first trimester. Short-term use may be considered in later pregnancy for distressing cough — discuss with pharmacist.
🤱 BFLimited data on transfer to breast milk. Honey and non-medicated lozenges preferred for cough.
Avoid combination cold/flu products containing multiple ingredients — risk-benefit is harder to assess. Single-ingredient products are always preferred.
Guaifenesin (expectorant)
e.g. Robitussin Chesty Cough
Avoid first trimester
🤰 PregAvoid in first trimester (some animal data of concern). Limited human data. May be used cautiously in second/third trimester if benefit clear.
🤱 BFLimited data. Staying hydrated is equally effective for most people.
Loratadine
e.g. Claratyne
Preferred antihistamine
🤰 PregPreferred oral antihistamine in pregnancy. Non-sedating. Most studied of the second-generation antihistamines. TGA Category B1.
🤱 BFLow transfer to breast milk. Considered compatible. Monitor infant for any unusual drowsiness.
Cetirizine
e.g. Zyrtec
Generally suitable
🤰 PregConsidered acceptable. Non-sedating second-generation antihistamine. TGA Category B2. Loratadine is often preferred as first choice due to slightly more safety data.
🤱 BFSome transfer to breast milk. Generally considered compatible. Monitor infant for drowsiness.
Promethazine / older antihistamines
e.g. Phenergan
Use with caution
🤰 PregSedating first-generation antihistamines. Generally avoided in late pregnancy due to neonatal effects. TGA Category C. Prefer loratadine.
🤱 BFAvoid — sedating antihistamines are associated with infant apnoea risk in breastfed babies, especially newborns and premature infants.
Important: Sedating antihistamines (e.g. promethazine) should be avoided in breastfeeding mothers of newborns or premature babies due to risk of infant apnoea.
Intranasal corticosteroids
e.g. Nasonex, Flixonase
Preferred for allergic rhinitis
🤰 PregBudesonide nasal spray is the preferred intranasal corticosteroid in pregnancy (most safety data). Considered low risk with minimal systemic absorption.
🤱 BFMinimal systemic absorption. All intranasal corticosteroids considered compatible with breastfeeding.

Sleep disturbance & anxiety

Sleep difficulties are very common in pregnancy. Non-pharmacological strategies should always be the first approach: sleep hygiene, relaxation techniques, cognitive behavioural therapy for insomnia (CBTi), and psychological support. If symptoms are severe, refer to a GP or mental health professional.

Melatonin
e.g. Circadin (now Rx in AU)
Limited data
🤰 PregMelatonin is produced naturally, but supplemental doses have limited safety data in pregnancy. Now prescription-only in Australia. Refer to GP.
🤱 BFNatural melatonin is present in breast milk. Supplemental melatonin data is limited. Discuss with GP before use.
Diphenhydramine
e.g. Restavit, Unisom
Avoid
🤰 PregOTC sleep aids containing sedating antihistamines. Not recommended as a routine sleep aid in pregnancy — especially late pregnancy. Avoid without GP guidance.
🤱 BFAvoid — sedating antihistamines in breast milk may cause infant apnoea, especially in newborns and premature babies.
Lavender / aromatherapy
Topical/diffuser use
Topical use generally safe
🤰 PregAromatherapy with lavender (external use, well-ventilated space) is generally considered low risk. Avoid ingestion of essential oils.
🤱 BFTopical or diffused lavender considered low risk. Avoid applying essential oils directly to nipples or areas in contact with infant's skin/mouth.
Never ingest essential oils. High concentration topical use should be avoided near the infant's face.

Herbal products
What to know before using herbal remedies during pregnancy or breastfeeding

⚠️ Remember: natural does not mean safe

Many patients assume herbal products are safe because they are "natural." However, herbal products can contain pharmacologically active compounds that may affect the baby, interact with medicines, or stimulate uterine contractions. Always tell your pharmacist about any herbal products you are using.

Ginger
Zingiber officinale
Pregnancy: generally safe Breastfeeding: likely safe
Evidence supports use for NVP at ≤1 g/day. Culinary amounts are unquestionably safe. High-dose supplements have limited data. Avoid doses above 1 g/day in pregnancy without advice.
Echinacea
Echinacea purpurea / angustifolia
Pregnancy: uncertain Breastfeeding: uncertain
Commonly used for cold/flu prevention. Limited human data in pregnancy. Some studies suggest it is low risk in first trimester, but evidence is insufficient to make a clear recommendation. Advise caution and GP discussion.
St John's Wort
Hypericum perforatum
Pregnancy: avoid Breastfeeding: avoid
May interact with many medicines (serotonin syndrome risk, reduces efficacy of oral contraceptives, anticoagulants). Associated with neonatal withdrawal symptoms. Transfers to breast milk and may cause infant colic, drowsiness, or poor feeding.
Raspberry leaf
Rubus idaeus
Pregnancy: avoid until term Breastfeeding: uncertain
Traditionally used to "prepare" the uterus for labour in late pregnancy. May stimulate uterine contractions — do not use before 37 weeks. No robust clinical evidence for efficacy. Advise against use in first and second trimester.
Chamomile
Matricaria chamomilla
Pregnancy: caution Breastfeeding: caution
Occasional chamomile tea (food amounts) is generally considered low risk. Concentrated supplements or frequent high-dose use should be avoided — emmenagogue properties reported at high doses. Limited safety data for breastfeeding.
Fenugreek
Trigonella foenum-graecum
Pregnancy: avoid Breastfeeding: caution
Widely used by breastfeeding mothers to boost milk supply, but evidence is mixed and it has uterine-stimulating properties — avoid in pregnancy. May cause GI side effects in mother and infant. Discuss with a pharmacist before use.
Peppermint
Mentha × piperita
Pregnancy: food amounts OK Breastfeeding: caution
Peppermint tea/food in normal amounts is generally considered safe. High-dose peppermint oil supplements should be avoided. Peppermint may reduce milk supply — often advised to avoid during breastfeeding, particularly concentrated forms.
Valerian
Valeriana officinalis
Pregnancy: avoid Breastfeeding: avoid
Commonly used for sleep and anxiety. Insufficient safety data in pregnancy and breastfeeding. Some alkaloids may be teratogenic at high doses in animal models. Advise avoidance until safer alternatives are explored.
Garlic (supplement doses)
Allium sativum
Food amounts: safe High-dose supplements: caution
Dietary garlic is safe and has no concerns. High-dose garlic supplements may inhibit platelet aggregation — advise against use near delivery. Limited breastfeeding data at supplement doses; may affect milk flavour.
Slippery elm
Ulmus rubra
Pregnancy: avoid Breastfeeding: uncertain
Sometimes used for reflux or nausea. Traditionally regarded as a potential abortifacient — insufficient evidence to establish safety in pregnancy. Advise against use until more data is available.

Understanding TGA pregnancy categories
A reference for interpreting Australian medicine classifications

Important limitation

TGA categories describe risk based on available evidence but do not account for trimester-specific risks, maternal dose, duration of use, or individual patient factors. They are a starting point — not a complete safety assessment. The TGA itself advises that these categories should not be the sole basis for clinical decisions.

Category A

Drugs taken by a large number of pregnant women without proven increase in malformations or harmful effects. Examples: paracetamol, folic acid, iron.

Category B1 / B2 / B3

Drugs taken by limited numbers of pregnant women. No increased frequency of malformations observed in humans. Animal data varies (B1 = no harm; B2 = limited animal data; B3 = some animal harm but unclear human relevance). Examples: loratadine (B1), cetirizine (B2), famotidine (B1).

Category C

Drugs that have caused or may be suspected of causing reversible effects in the fetus without causing malformations. Long-term effects uncertain. These should only be used if benefit outweighs risk. Examples: promethazine, some decongestants.

Category D

Evidence of increased risk of fetal malformations or irreversible damage. May still be used in life-threatening conditions. Examples: some antiepileptics, ACE inhibitors in second/third trimester.

Category X

High risk of permanent damage. Contraindicated in pregnancy. Examples: thalidomide, isotretinoin (Roaccutane), misoprostol (for abortion induction).

Reliable resources
For patients and healthcare professionals in Australia

Mothersafe (NSW)

A clinical service providing expert advice on medicine and substance exposure during pregnancy and breastfeeding. Pharmacists and patients can call for guidance.

📞 1800 647 848

LactMed (NLM)

Free US National Library of Medicine database covering drug and supplement safety in breastfeeding. Highly detailed, evidence-based, regularly updated.

🌐 toxnet.nlm.nih.gov/lactmed

e-Therapeutics+ / AMH

Australian Medicines Handbook — preferred professional reference for Australian context. Includes pregnancy and lactation guidance for most medicines.

MIMS Online

Australian drug reference database. Product information leaflets include pregnancy and lactation details from the manufacturer.

Pregnancy, Birth & Baby

Australian Government-backed service with midwife and nurse support for parents and expectant families.

📞 1800 882 436

Australian Prescriber

Peer-reviewed, free-access journal with articles on medicine safety in pregnancy and lactation. Useful for in-depth clinical reading.
